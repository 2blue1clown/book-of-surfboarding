A way to classify turns

Credit: Jazz Berlin, David Cox

Defining surfing maneuvers is a frustrating task. There is lots of overlap and inconsistency in classifications that I have found on the internet. For example the turn Lakey Peterson calls a snap Martin Dunn calls a reentry. This exercise is not about determining who is right or wrong, I just want to define turns for myself.

Every wave is a different shape, power, speed and size. You can perform turns in multiple places on the wave and that will effect the how critical/difficult each turn is.

To define turns we need to have an understanding of the different locations/features of a wave. We need to know that the pocket is the steepest part of the wave. We need to know that the lip is the crest of the wave about to break. We need to know that the shoulder is further along the wave, farther than the pocket.

The ideas for this classification were in Martin Dunn’s tutorial videos and distilled into these useful diagrams by The Wave’s local surf guru Jazz Berlin. Ideally this will provide a vocabulary for talking about the shape of the turn, leaving room for words to describe frills. For example a layback half-snap and a grab-rail half-snap will look different but the surfer will be traveling through the same positions on the wave.

Re-entry

A re-entry (also known as a rio) occurs when the surfer does a steep bottom turn all the way up into the lip, sharply rotates his board and then re-enters the wave.

A large number of people call this a snap, but for the purpose of my classification it will be a reentry so that I can reasonably talk about what follows.

Variants: Blow-tail, tail-slide, layback

Snap

This one is frustrating for me, so here are some definitions from people on the internet.

The snap, also known as slash, is a radical change of trajectory in the pocket or the top of the wave. When performed abruptly, it produces spectacular, flashy buckets of spray above the surfer. (Luis M. Pinto)
A snap is a sharper and more aggressive version of carving, and it's basically a change of direction. However, one thing to keep in mind is that it can only be performed on a steep wave, and besides speed and body coordination, it requires a perfected bottom turn. (Jeremy Dean)
The forehand snap is a rail turn performed in the pocket sections of waves where speed is maintained throughout. This maintenance of speed is created by the surfer looking back to the foam, which is turn allows the full rotation of the arms, upper body and hips creating a drive force through the turn. (Martin Dunn)

Martin Dunn talks about full snaps and half snaps, and this refers to how far the board comes around by the end of the turn. The easiest way to think about it is:

Full snaps go back to the pocket.

Half snaps go back to the trough.

Cutback

A cutback is when you do a shallower bottom turn out to the shoulder, and then change direction to come back to the foam.

Cutbacks and snaps have some overlap depending on where you define the pocket and shoulder of the wave to be.

Variants: layback, soul-arch, grab-rail

Roundhouse Cutback

Roundhouse cutbacks are cutbacks that have a re-entry performed as the surfer rebounds off the foam.

Variants: None!

Variants: Lip-line float
